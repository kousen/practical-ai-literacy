# Practical AI Literacy

**A half-day, hands-on workshop for the Trinity College community**

This workshop helps participants move from knowing the AI vocabulary to using these tools well in their actual work. It is hands-on and practical rather than a debate about whether AI is good or bad. Two ideas run throughout: the important question is no longer *which* tool to use but *how* to use it, and because the tools keep changing, the durable skill is learning to evaluate them yourself.

## Getting Started: What AI Literacy Means Now

- The shift from "which tool should I use" to "how should I use it"  
- Why these tools keep changing — and why that, not any single tool, is the real lesson  
- Opening demo: turning speech into clean text faster than you can type

## How AI Tools Actually Work

- Models, prompts, tokens, and context windows  
- Conversations and memory  
  - Why tools sometimes forget, drift, or confidently invent information  
  - What a "context window" means in practice  
- Commercial tools vs. open-weights models  
  - ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini  
  - Smaller models you can run yourself (Gemma, Qwen)  
- Cost literacy: subscriptions, input/output token pricing, and what happens at scale

## Working Conversationally with AI

- Iteration, not one-shot queries: ask, refine, challenge, and add context  
- Generation as well as summarization  
  - Reports, recommendations, agendas, emails, rubrics, and first drafts  
- Using AI to absorb tedious, repetitive, and blank-page work  
- Prompting that actually helps: examples, context, step-by-step reasoning, and format requests

## Hands-On Lab: From Messy Notes to Useful Output

- Start from raw, disorganized notes  
- Produce a polished report, email, agenda, or summary  
- Refine the result through conversation rather than accepting the first draft  
- Compare how different tools handle the same task

## Connected AI and Your Own Data

- Microsoft Copilot grounded in your own Trinity email and documents, inside the institution's privacy boundary  
- Connectors as bridges between AI tools and outside data or systems  
- Cowork-style tools and "skills" for non-specialists  
  - Drafting, review, and structured analysis  
- Live example: analyzing realistic but entirely invented data (e.g., a student-aid scenario)  
- Where this is heading: agents, MCP servers, and AI coding assistants

## Multimodal AI

- Image generation and editing  
- Vision models: reading and analyzing documents, slides, screenshots, and photos  
- Voice tools for dictation and transcription (Wispr Flow)  
- The fast-emerging world of AI video generation and editing

## Using AI Without Fooling Yourself

- Spotting confident, wrong answers  
  - Hands-on "catch the hallucination" exercise  
- Building verification into your workflow  
  - Cross-checking one tool's output with a second tool  
- Privacy and confidentiality  
  - Student records, donor data, and anything covered by FERPA  
  - Why sensitive institutional data never goes into consumer tools  
- Equity: who has paid tools and who doesn't, among both employees and students  
- For instructors: classroom use, assessment, and AI detection as evolving problems, not solved ones

## Wrap-Up

- Each participant identifies one workflow to try and one risk or question to manage  
- Continuing the conversation: the AI Lab's ongoing sessions at the Elting Innovation and Entrepreneurship Center
